Creating the Redistricting Model

Things to Know Before Starting

Before getting involved in the redistricting process, there are a few things you should know about the current proposal. These things include, but are not limited to:

  • Who created the proposal currently being propsed by your school district?
    • Members of the school board?
    • Third party consultants?
    • Parents and school faculty?
  • Do the proposed maps make sense?
    • Do they follow known traffic patterns or obvious district/neighborhood boundaries?
    • Does it consider strong knit communities or geographical proximity? I.e. does it cut out people who are within walking distance of a specific school out of that school district?

Presenting Your Proposal

Tips and Important Considerations for Presenting Your Case

When creating your proposal presentation, metntion the changes to the current proposal that lead to your final point for a specific school. For example, if you are talking about the percent utilization of a specific school, talk about the changes you made or things you are keeping (i.e. your assumptions) that lead to your estimation of the percent utilization. In addtion, mention clearly where the plan agrees and disagrees with the school district's current redistricting plans, and why you made or did make changes. Lastly, state clearly your purpose for proposing the altered redistricting plan. Think: GOALS, CRITERIA!

Everything Else

If specific schools have special considerations mention that in your proposal and account for it in your alternative plan. The plan should account for everything pre and post changes. Considerations are things such as school renovation. If the capacity of a school will change after the renovations are completed, account for the numbers before and after the renovation and include that in your proposal.

Do not ignore the current proposed redistricting plan. Many of the examples we provide show the differences between the parent lead redistricting proposal and the redistricting proposal given by the school district. Your argument for change will be stronger if the school board sees that you considered their proposal in the creation of your own.
